# Military art references

The production military sheets depict a broad mid-to-late eighteenth-century
visual language rather than one campaign or regiment.  Their shared rendering
target is the game's existing Kriegsspiel-table art direction: readable
national and unit-class silhouettes, museum-miniature material depth, and one
warm upper-left light source.

## England

- Musketeers use scarlet coats, blue facings, white crossbelts, cocked hats,
  black gaiters, and Long Land Pattern-style flintlocks.  The [Royal
  Armouries' Brown Bess history](https://royalarmouries.org/objects-and-stories/stories/gendering-the-armouries)
  dates the Land Service Pattern to 1730–31 and identifies the Long Land
  Pattern as the British Army's principal arm in the War of the Austrian
  Succession.
- The close detail and facing treatment follows surviving eighteenth-century
  uniform studies such as the National Army Museum's [1780 militia grenadier
  painting](https://collection.nam.ac.uk/detail.php?acc=1966-11-20-1).
- The polearm unit is represented as a sergeant or guard carrying a partisan,
  not a medieval armoured pikeman.  The Metropolitan Museum's [British
  partisan](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/34306) provides the
  period silhouette and material reference.
- Stable troops are late-century light dragoons in crested leather helmets,
  avoiding the tall fur busby and pelisse of Britain's later formal hussars.
  National Army Museum collection records include a [15th Light Dragoons
  helmet dated 1770](https://collection.nam.ac.uk/inventory/objects/results.php?page=2&unit=15th),
  while its [10th Hussars history](https://collection.nam.ac.uk/detail.php?acc=2017-10-32-1)
  notes that the unit was still light dragoons from 1783 and did not become
  hussars until 1806.
- The field piece follows the Royal Artillery Museum's description of the
  [smoothbore 6-pounder](https://www.royalartillerymuseum.com/our-collection/waterloo-gun)
  as a standard light field gun from the mid-1700s, including the lighter
  block-trail carriage introduced in the 1770s.

## Ottoman Empire

- Infantry dress draws on British Museum costume studies: the [Janissary in
  common dress in 1700](https://www.britishmuseum.org/collection/object/P_1871-0812-5097)
  and an [Ottoman Janissary costume study from
  1790](https://www.britishmuseum.org/collection/object/W_1974-0617-0-12-1-91).
  These establish the wrapped headwear, layered mantle or kaftan, loose
  trousers, broad sash, and sidearm.
- The long guns are miquelet flintlocks with restrained inlay.  The
  Metropolitan Museum's [Ottoman gun dated
  1784–85](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/30605) documents the
  army's late-seventeenth-century move from matchlocks to this mechanism and
  the characteristic steel, wood, bone, copper-alloy, and gold materials.
- Sipahi tack and saddlecloth ornament take their material cues from the Met's
  [Ottoman saddle](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/22398) and
  [silver-inlaid Ottoman ring bit](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/29353).
- Artillery shares the infantry's layered dress while distinguishing the
  foundry unit through an engraved bronze tube, practical field carriage,
  sponge, rammer, vent tool, and two-person crew.

The checked-in procedural painters remain load-failure fallbacks.  Production
frames are baked into the left/right unit atlases once at battle start, so the
runtime still performs one image blit per visible unit.
