# Ottoman architecture art direction

The Ottoman production set depicts a prosperous late-seventeenth- to
eighteenth-century military settlement rather than treating every civic
building as a mosque. The forms share one material language while keeping each
building's economic or military function legible at gameplay scale.

## Material and form decisions

- Civic stonework uses pale limestone and ashlar, brick relieving courses,
  alternating stone-and-brick arches, deep arcades, lead-covered domes, carved
  timber bays, red clay roof tiles, and restrained blue geometric tile bands.
- The Town Center is an administrative divan and palace pavilion: a domed
  audience block, arcaded reception front, broad stair, roof terrace, and
  timber oriel identify government without turning the structure into a
  religious complex.
- Houses use plastered masonry lower floors with timber-framed upper stories,
  broad eaves, shutters, roof tiles, small courtyards, and climbing vegetation.
- The mill, lumber camp, mine, stable, barracks, foundry, and tower expose their
  work: sails and millstones, saws and stacked timber, winding gear and ore
  carts, open stalls and tack, long barrack arcades, cannon moulds and furnace
  glow, and a crenellated gun platform.
- Fortifications use battered limestone bases, brick string courses, narrow
  firing slits, turquoise geometric panels, weathered lower masonry, timber
  doors, domed gate turrets, and build-state scaffolding. Open and barred gates
  have separate authored silhouettes in both board directions.
- Geometric ornament is kept nonfigural and subordinate to structure so the
  buildings remain readable as military-game pieces rather than decorative
  icons.

## Historical anchors

- [The Met: The Art of the Ottomans after 1600](https://www.metmuseum.org/essays/the-art-of-the-ottomans-after-1600)
  provides the broad period vocabulary and continuity of Ottoman court art.
- [UNESCO: Historic Areas of Istanbul](https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/356/)
  anchors the monumental stone, domed, arcaded, and palace traditions of the
  imperial capital.
- [UNESCO: Nuruosmaniye Complex](https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/6122/)
  provides a mid-eighteenth-century upper bound for the set's curved details,
  carved stone richness, and synthesis of established Ottoman forms with newer
  Baroque movement.
- [The Met: Geometric Patterns in Islamic Art](https://www.metmuseum.org/essays/geometric-patterns-in-islamic-art)
  informs the restrained tile panels and repeating nonfigural bands.

The checked-in art is original production artwork. References establish period
materials, structure, and ornament; no museum or heritage-site image is copied
into the game.
